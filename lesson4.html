Lesson 4:

Case Study – Lumut, Perak & What We Found

Objectives

  • Describe the study area (Taman Paya Bakau) and its significance
  • Summarize the timeline, data sources, and methods used (1990, 2000, 2017)
  • Interpret key findings on mangrove loss, health changes, and drivers
  • Explain how these insights guide local conservation and restoration actions

Spotlight on Taman Paya Bakau, Lumut

Map of Manjung District highlighting Taman Paya Bakau

  • Location: Urban-fringe park between Lumut town and Bandar Seri Manjung, Perak.
  • Size: ~200 ha of continuous mangrove forest, boardwalks, and educational trails.
  • Why it matters:
    • Only coastal mangrove park fully managed by Manjung Municipal Council.
    • Live classroom for students, NGOs, eco-tourism, and community planting programs.

Data & Methods Recap

Year Satellite & Sensor Key Processing Steps
1990 Landsat-5 TM (30 m) Geometric & radiometric correction → NDVI → supervised classification
2000 Landsat-7 ETM+ (30 m) Cloud masking → index stacking → unsupervised classification
2017 Landsat-8 OLI (30 m) & RapidEye (5 m) High-res mosaic → NDVI trend analysis → change detection

GIS Platforms: ArcGIS Pro for map layouts; Google Earth Engine for batch processing and NDVI time series.
Ground Truth: GPS points and on-site health surveys in 2018–19 to validate classification accuracy (±5 % error).

What We Found

1. Extent Changes

  • Total mangrove loss (1990→2017): 15 ha (7.5 % of park)
  • Hotspots of deforestation:
    • Southern edge near port expansion
    • Northern fringe by new coastal road

Changes of mangroves that occurred between 1990 and 2017 overlaid on GIS platform

2. Health & Density Trends (NDVI)

  • Average NDVI drop: from 0.62 (1990) → 0.54 (2017)
  • Recovery patches: small gains (+0.1 NDVI) in replanting zones (2010–15)
  • Decline patches: steep NDVI fall (–0.15) near pollution discharge points

3. Key Drivers

  • Coastal Development: Port facilities & road widening encroaching on mangroves
  • Pollution: Runoff from urban areas boosting salinity & chemicals
  • Natural Disturbance: Monsoon storms (2005, 2014) uprooting trees in seaward zone

From Data to Decisions

  • Protected Zones: 50 m buffer designated where no development is allowed.
  • Restoration Priorities: Replanting in northern fringe (NDVI 0.4–0.5).
  • Water Quality Monitoring: Monthly salinity & pollutant checks at discharge sites.
  • Community Engagement: “Adopt-a-Plot”—schools monitor 1 ha plots, record NDVI via apps.

Outcome: Annual reports show a 3 ha net gain by 2020 in restored zones and rising community stewardship.
