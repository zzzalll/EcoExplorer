What Are Mangroves & Why They Matter

Mangroves 101: Definition & Habitat

What is a mangrove?

A mangrove is not a single tree but a community of salt-tolerant trees and shrubs.
They grow where freshwater meets the sea—in river mouths, estuaries, and sheltered bays.

Key environmental factors:

- Salinity: Can handle brackish (mixed) water.
- Tides: Roots adapted to daily flooding.
- Muddy substrate: Oxygen-poor soil.


Mangrove “zones”:

  • Seaward edge (often Sonneratia alba)
    • Constantly flooded, thick trunks up to 13 m tall

  • Mid-zone (e.g., Rhizophora apiculata)
    • Characterized by “prop roots” arching into the mud

  • Landward fringe (e.g., Avicennia alba)
    • High pneumatophores (air roots) poking straight up

Analogy: Think of a mangrove forest as a three-layer cake:

  • The bottom sponge (sea edge)
  • The creamy filling (middle zone)
  • The frosting on top (landward fringe)

Ecosystem Services: Why Mangroves Matter

  1. Coastal Protection
    2.
    • Roots and trunks break waves and slow storm surge—“nature’s breakwater.”

  2. Nursery Grounds
    3.
    • Shallow, sheltered waters let juvenile fish, crabs, and shrimp grow safely

  3. Carbon Storage
    4.
    • Mangroves lock huge amounts of carbon in their soils—“blue carbon” champions

  4. Water Filter
    5.
    • Roots trap sediments, absorb pollutants, and purify runoff before it hits the sea

Local Spotlight: Malaysia & Lumut

Malaysia’s mangroves cover about 630 000 hectors — over a third in Sabah, 22% in Sarawak, 18% in Peninsular Malaysia

Lumut’s gem: Taman Paya Bakau

  • The only urban mangrove park in Malaysia, tucked between Lumut town and Bandar Seri Manjung
  • A living classroom for students, fishermen, and visitors who plant seedlings and learn about coastal ecology

Did you know? Nearly half of Malaysia’s fish catch depends on mangrove-linked nurseries
